## POST TEXT

We built a hiring process before we wrote a single line of product code.

Not a team-building exercise. A real process. Role files, competency tests, a constitution, an
access matrix, an HR director, and a security scan -- for a company with zero customers and
zero revenue.

Here is the part I still find funny: the first "employee" we hired was HR. An AI HR director.
Because before you can hire anyone, you need someone to run hiring. And in a company built
entirely on AI agents, even that someone is an AI.

So there I was, in the first week, interviewing an AI so it could interview the other AIs.

It sounds like a recursive joke. It kind of is. But it also forced something useful. We had
to decide early: are these agents tools, or are they something more like teammates? Tools you
use. Teammates you hold accountable.

We chose teammates.

That meant every agent needed a job description (we called them role files), a skills test,
a manager sign-off, and owner approval before going live. It meant a constitution -- actual
rules about what any agent can and cannot do, under any circumstance.

It meant that by the time we ran a single line of real product code, the company had 11 agents
certified and operational, a full governance stack, and an audit log.

Was it overkill for a two-week-old startup? Maybe.

But when you are building with autonomous AI agents, the decisions you make in week one become
the culture. And culture is much harder to fix than code.

This is the story of how Eco-Synthetic was built. Starting from the beginning.

Follow along -- the next posts get weirder from here.
